Accu-Cam
Senior Design Project

Accu-cam was my senior design project I created along with three other people. The camera is designed to be used for teachers and speakers in online meetings so they're able to move freely throughout their space to elevate online learning. Our design won first place as the best invention at the University of Pittsburgh's engineering design expo with all of the engineering majors showcasing their final projects. The camera will track the speaker by rotating a motor the camera is attached to. The laptop it's attached too will also create a transcript of what the speaker said. I was responsible for multithreading, video capture, natural language processing, and helped with the facial recognition and angle calculations. All of the programming was done in Python.

Facial Recognition

The Haar-Cascade classifier is optimal for simple and lightweight image classification and facial recognition. This classifier is optimal for a fully independent implementation of a CV model as it requires little processing power to operate effectively on a small device like the Raspberry Pi Zero. The baseline theory relies on applying simple masks images to identify features in the image. These masks slide across the image in order to find the assigned feature. This results in a fast and robust image recognition algorithm. In addition to this, we selected this classifier since it is natively trained in the OpenCV library.

Natural Language Processing

The Google Speech Recognition API is a cloud-based NLP model trained on a variety of dialects and accents for English speakers. Aside from the technical specifications, this model was chosen for its large vocabulary and portability. With it being online, users require an internet connection. However, it is expected for them to have this since they will be using Web Conferencing software while it’s in use. It is also found in the “Speech Recognition” Python library, making it easily accessible.

Angle Calculation

The algorithm for calculating the angle directive for the motor utilizes the found width and position from the Haar-Cascade Classifier. The algorithm also operates upon the calculated value for the focal length, using the following formula: f=d(wImage/wReal). With this, we can solve for the angle by applying the Pythagorean theorem with the inverse sine and then send the angle to the USB. Motor

The motor control module comprised three hardware components, such as the Rapsberry Pi 4, USB to serial adapter, and servo motor. In summary, the USB to serial adapter transmitted data serial from the user’s PC to the Raspberry Pi 4 which then processed the step angle to drive the servo motor.

A micro servo motor (SG90) was found to be suitable hardware for control due to its closed loop system and inexpensive price. This servo motor operated at a voltage ranging from 4.8 V to 6 V. In the final implemented design, the servo had a minimum and maximum sweep from 0º to 180º. The servo had three terminals for ground, power, and pulse width modulation (PWM). Both power and ground terminal were directly connected to a voltage regulator PCB which was powered by a 9V battery. The servo was controlled by GPIO pin 25 of the Raspberry Pi 4 which was connected to the PWM pin of the servo.
